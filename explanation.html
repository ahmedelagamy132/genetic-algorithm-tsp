Genetic algorithm · Travelling Salesman

Evolution as a search strategy

The instrument breeds better and better delivery routes the way nature breeds better organisms: keep what works, recombine it, mutate it, repeat. Here is exactly what is happening behind the glowing line.

The problem

A salesman must visit every city once and return home by the shortest possible route. Easy to state, brutal to solve. With n cities there are (n−1)! / 2 distinct tours.

At 10 cities that is about 181 000 routes: a computer checks them instantly. At just 22 cities, the default here, it is roughly 1.4 × 1019 — more tours than seconds since the dinosaurs. You cannot try them all. You need to search intelligently, and that is what a genetic algorithm does.

The encoding

A genetic algorithm borrows four ideas from biology. To use it on any problem you decide what a "creature" is and what makes one fitter than another.

Chromosome an individual
One candidate tour: an ordered list of cities, e.g. [4, 1, 7, 2, …]. Every city appears exactly once, so a tour is a permutation.
Fitness how good
Shorter is better, so fitness is 1 / tour length. The whole population is judged on this single number.
Population the gene pool
A few hundred tours held at once. Diversity here is what lets the search escape dead ends.
Generation one cycle
Selection, crossover and mutation produce a fresh population from the current one. Repeat thousands of times.

1 · Selection — survival of the fitter

To choose a parent, pick k tours at random and keep the shortest. This is tournament selection. A larger k (the selection pressure slider) means fitter parents win more often: the search converges faster but explores less, risking a merely-good local optimum. Small k keeps underdogs in play and the search broad.

2 · Crossover — recombination

Two parent tours produce a child that inherits structure from both. You cannot just splice halves: that would visit some cities twice and skip others. Order crossover (OX) keeps one parent's slice intact, then fills the rest in the second parent's order, so the child is always a valid tour.

Brass cells are copied from A; cool cells are filled from B in its own order, skipping anything already present.

PMX (partially mapped crossover) is an alternative that swaps a slice and then repairs the duplicates it creates through a position mapping. Both are offered so you can watch them behave differently on the same map.

3 · Mutation — the spark of novelty

Without mutation the population quickly goes stale: every child is a remix of genes that already exist. Mutation injects new structure.

  • Inversion reverses a random segment of the tour. Geometrically this uncrosses a tangle of routes, which is why it is so effective on the map. It is the default.
  • Swap simply exchanges two stops. Gentler and more random; useful to see how a weaker operator slows convergence.

Turn the mutation rate to zero and the run freezes at whatever it had; turn it too high and good tours get shredded faster than selection can save them. The sweet spot is the lesson.

4 · Elitism — never lose the best

Crossover and mutation are gambles, so the very best tours are copied into the next generation untouched. With elitism on, the brass "best so far" line can only ever go down. Set elitism to zero and watch the best wobble as good solutions are occasionally lost.

Reading the instrument

  • The brass line on the map is the best tour found so far. Faint lines behind it are other members of the population still searching.
  • The convergence chart plots the best tour (brass) and the population average (cool) over generations. A widening gap means the elite are pulling ahead of the pack.
  • Stagnant counts generations since the last improvement — a sign the search has settled, or is stuck.
  • Pin run freezes the current convergence curve as a dashed ghost. Change a parameter, reset, and race the new run against it. Because the cities are seeded, the comparison is fair.
An honest caveat

A genetic algorithm finds a very good tour, not a provably optimal one. For a live, visual, tunable search across an astronomically large space, that trade is exactly the point: you can watch a sensible answer emerge in seconds.
